Excited for the 2018 NBA Draft?

Evaluating the historical drafting performances of NBA franchises to determine how excited one should be for the upcoming draft.

As an almost lifelong Pistons fan (I'm ashamed to say I didn't have the strength to stick it through 2010-2015), I've come to hate the NBA draft. While the Pistons have had some superb picks in the past, my lifetime has been filled with disappointment after disappointment. Most recently, we passed up on Donovan Mitchell for Luke Kennard, took Stanley Johnson while Devin Booker was still on the board, selected Brandon Knight over Kemba Walker, and of course infamously drafted kickboxer Darko Milicic with the second pick in the stacked 2003 NBA draft over the likes of Carmelo Anthony, Chris Bosh, and Dwyane Wade. Barring a miracle, the Pistons have no chance at redemption in the 2018 draft, having traded away their first round pick as part of the Blake Griffin deal.

With only the 42nd pick in this years draft, not much is expected out of the Pistons. The average lifetime win shares produced by a 42nd pick is a paltry 5.26 wins.

However, a bunch of fanbases in the lottery are delighted at the opportunity to land a player that can lead their team to the promised land. Can they trust their teams to make the correct picks though? To answer this question, we can quantify each franchises historical drafting prowess by creating a "draft score" for each picked player.

Draft Score = Drafted Player's Lifetime Win Shares - Average Lifetime Win Shares at Drafted Player's Pick Number
A "draft score" is calculated for every single draft pick (prior to 1989, there are more than 2 rounds, but only the top 60 picks are evaluated). This draft score is calculated by taking the difference between the draft pick's lifetime win shares produced and the average lifetime win shares produced by a player at that pick. Because we are using lifetime win shares, I've removed the last 5 years of drafts, as these players haven't had sufficient time to make their mark on the league. The drafts evaluated are: 1979 (first year of the 3-point line) to 2013.

The draft scores aim to measure the value added by each pick, while taking into account the opportunity that the franchise was given. From the boxplots, we can see that for the most part, the IQR of all the franchises are very similar, and the only major differences lie in the franchise-altering outliers (both high and low). This fully supports Sam Hinkie's theory that the drafting is an imperfect practice, and the only effective way to "beat the system" is by stockpiling more opportunities to pick early in the draft. The previously shown line chart shows that the average number of lifetime win shares drops off significantly after the top 5 picks. Despite this, some franchises, such as Oklahoma City, haven't picked a single player with a draft score greater than 100, yet have the highest cumulative draft score. This means that they consistently pick well, and either select great prospects or develop them well. As a note, you may have noticed Kobe Bryant showing up as the Hornets pick, or Dirk Nowitzki showing up on the Bucks. Due to the data source that was used, draft-day trades were not taken into account.
