Excited for the 2018 NBA Draft?

Evaluating the historical drafting performances of NBA franchises to determine how excited each fanbase should be for the upcoming draft.

As an almost lifelong Pistons fan (I'm ashamed to say I didn't have the strength to stick it through 2010-2015), I've come to hate the NBA draft. While the Pistons have had some superb picks in the 80s and 90s, my lifetime has been filled with disappointment after disappointment. Most recently, we passed up on Donovan Mitchell for Luke Kennard, took Stanley Johnson while Devin Booker was still on the board, selected Brandon Knight over Kemba Walker, and of course infamously drafted kickboxer Darko Milicic with the second pick in the stacked 2003 NBA draft over the likes of Carmelo Anthony, Chris Bosh, and Dwyane Wade. Barring a miracle, the Pistons have no chance at redemption in the 2018 draft, having traded away their first round pick as part of the Blake Griffin deal. The Pistons have been left with the 42nd pick in this years draft.

Having traded away their first round pick as part of the Blake Griffin deal, the Pistons have been left with only the 42nd pick in the 2018 draft. Barring a miracle, the Pistons have no chance at redemption this year, as the average lifetime win shares produced by a 42nd pick is a paltry 5.26 wins.

While Pistons fans don't have much to get excited about in the upcoming draft, a bunch of fanbases in the lottery are delighted at the opportunity to land a transformative player that can lead their team to the promised land. Can they trust in their teams to make the right picks though? To answer this question, we can quantify each franchises historical drafting prowess by creating a "draft score" for each pick.

Draft Score = Drafted Player's Lifetime Win Shares - Average Lifetime Win Shares at Drafted Player's Pick Number

A "draft score" is calculated for every single draft pick (prior to 1989, there were more than 2 rounds, but only the top 60 picks are evaluated). This draft score is calculated by taking the difference between the draft pick's lifetime win shares produced and the average lifetime win shares produced by a player at that pick. This measures the value added by each pick, while taking into account the opportunity that the franchise was given.

Because we are using lifetime win shares, I've removed the last 5 years of drafts, as these players haven't had sufficient time to make their mark on the league. The drafts evaluated are: 1979 (first year of the 3-point line) to 2013.

*Draft day trades are not taken into account. Thus, Kobe Bryant is associated with the Hornets, Dirk Nowitzki is associated with the Bucks, etc.

When we plot out the picks, we can see that the vast majority of them across the league have a draft score between 15 and -15. This stems from the fact that the late first round picks and the entirety of the second round have a small variance. The picks that matter are the lottery picks, and the rest are unlikely to alter the states of their franchises.

Once we look outside the interquartile range, we can see the stars and busts in NBA history. Karl Malone and John Stockton stand out as some of the best picks ever, as they were selected towards the end of the lottery, yet both played 19 years in the league at an elite level. Michael Olowokandi and Greg Oden stand out for less desirable reasons. Both were taken first overall, but never once topped 5 win shares in a season.

By summing up the all the draft scores for each franchise, we're able to rank each franchise's success in the draft.

Franchises like OKC have consistently made picks that outperform expectations while avoiding any major busts. Other franchises near the top of the list are carried by a few crazy outliers that outweigh a good numer of underperforming picks.
